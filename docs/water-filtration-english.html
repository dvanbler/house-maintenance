Water Filtration System

Installation & Design Guide

Chi River House — Satuek, Buriram Province, Thailand

Water sources: Rainwater collection (primary) | Chi River water (dry season backup)

1. Water Sources & Characteristics

1.1 Primary Source: Rainwater

Rainwater is collected from a total of 2,782 sq ft (258 square metres) of roof area, comprising:

  • 924 sq ft — 50% of the main ceramic tile roof (42ft × 44ft house)
  • 1,438 sq ft — Thai kitchen metal roof extension (largest single collection surface)
  • 420 sq ft — rear extension metal roof

Water flows via gutters into five 2,000L ground-level storage tanks (10,000L total collection capacity). A float-activated transfer pump fills a sixth elevated 2,000L tank, which gravity-feeds the house supply line.

At the regional average annual rainfall of 1,200–1,400mm, this roof area collects approximately 248,000–289,000 litres per year — far exceeding tank capacity. The 12,000L tank storage provides approximately 9–10 days of full-occupancy supply when full, making the Chi River backup essential during extended dry periods.

Rainwater requires filtration for:

  • Sediment — dust, debris, bird droppings, and roof particles carried by gutters
  • Bacteria and pathogens — can develop in stored water in tropical heat
  • Organic matter — leaves, algae growth in tanks over time
  • Roof surface contaminants — paint particles, zinc from metal roofing

1.2 Dry Season Backup: Chi River Water (November – March)

During dry months when rainfall is insufficient, a mobile pump and hoses fill the collection tanks directly from the Chi River. River water requires more thorough filtration due to:

  • High sediment load — the Chi River carries significant suspended silt
  • Agricultural runoff — fertilizers and pesticides from surrounding rice paddies
  • Higher bacterial and pathogen load than rainwater
  • Possible organic compounds from upstream activity

⚠️ IMPORTANT: The filtration system must handle both water sources. River water is significantly more challenging and will clog filters faster. Plan for more frequent cartridge replacement during dry season river water periods (November–March).

2. Recommended Filtration System — Split Design

2.1 Why a Split Design?

Filtration is split across two locations: sediment filters before the elevated tank, and chemical/biological treatment after it. This is the correct approach because:

  • Sediment filters before the tank prevent silt and debris accumulating inside the elevated tank and clogging downstream filters
  • Carbon and UV treatment after the tank removes chemicals and kills any bacteria that grow during storage in the tank
  • UV placed before the tank would be ineffective — bacteria can regrow in stored water after UV treatment, especially in Thailand’s heat
  • The elevated tank itself should be opaque/dark to inhibit algae growth, and kept sealed with a fine mesh vent

2.2 Complete System Flow

Collection tanks → DC Solar Pump → Stage 1 (20μm Sediment) → Stage 2 (5μm Sediment)
    → Elevated Tank (2,000L)
        → Booster Pump
            → Stage 3 (Carbon Filter) → Stage 4 (UV Sterilizer)
                → House distribution
                    → RO Filter (kitchen drinking water only)

🔑 KEY POINT: Stages 1 & 2 (sediment) go BEFORE the elevated tank, pushed by the DC solar pump. The booster pump comes AFTER the elevated tank and pushes water through Stages 3 & 4 (carbon + UV) before reaching the house.

2.3 Stage-by-Stage Description

Stage Filter Type Location Purpose Replacement Priority
1 Sediment 20μm Before tank Removes large particles, silt, leaves, debris. Protects tank and downstream filters. Every 1–2 months (more often with river water) Essential
2 Sediment 5μm Before tank Removes finer particles that passed Stage 1. Protects carbon filter. Every 2–3 months Essential
3 Activated Carbon After tank Removes organic compounds, pesticides, agricultural chemicals, odors. Every 3–6 months Essential for river water
4 UV Sterilizer After tank Kills bacteria, viruses, and pathogens that may have grown during tank storage. No chemicals required. UV bulb annually Strongly recommended

NOTE: The existing reverse osmosis (RO) filter in the kitchen handles drinking water separately. The RO removes virtually all remaining contaminants and is not part of this system.

3. UV Sterilizer — Key Requirements

3.1 Flow Rate

The UV unit must be rated for the expected peak household flow rate. For a house with up to 10 guests, install a unit rated for at least 20–25 litres per minute (LPM).

UV Unit Size Flow Rate Suitable For
Small 8–12 LPM 1–2 simultaneous outlets
Medium 15–20 LPM 2–3 simultaneous outlets
Large 25–40 LPM Whole house, multiple simultaneous users — recommended

3.2 Water Clarity Requirement

Water entering the UV must be clear — ideally below 1 NTU turbidity. Cloudy or turbid water blocks UV light penetration and pathogens can survive. This is why the sediment pre-filters (Stages 1 and 2) are non-negotiable.

3.3 Power Consumption

A medium UV unit uses approximately 16–25 watts, only when running. As it only operates when the booster pump is active (when a tap is open), actual daily energy use is approximately 15 watt-hours — negligible on the solar system.

4. Elevated Tank — Best Practices

Since carbon and UV treatment happen after the elevated tank, the tank itself should be maintained carefully:

  • Use an opaque or dark-colored tank — sunlight promotes algae growth; darkness inhibits it
  • Keep the tank sealed with a fine mesh vent — prevents insects and debris from entering
  • Clean the interior annually — check for sediment buildup or algae
  • The float-activated transfer pump fills the tank automatically during daylight from the ground collection tanks

5. First Flush Diverter (Recommended)

Install a first flush diverter on each gutter downpipe feeding the collection tanks. This device automatically discards the first few litres of rainfall — which carry the most roof contamination — before allowing clean water into the tanks.

  • Significantly reduces sediment and contaminant load on all downstream filters
  • Extends filter cartridge life and reduces maintenance frequency
  • Very low cost — no power required

Install on each downpipe feeding the ground collection tanks.
